3. "Renting is throwing money away"Half true. Here's the honest version

This is the most repeated piece of advice about buying a home, and it's only half right. Let's be precise about it, because it's a big decision.

What's true about it

When you pay rent, that money is gone. When you pay a mortgage, part of each payment pays down what you owe — that part becomes equity, which is real money you get back when you sell. Over years, that adds up. And if the house gains value, that gain is yours too.

What nobody tells you

A big chunk of a mortgage payment is also money you never see again:

InterestIn the early years this is most of your payment. On a $300k loan at 6.5%, about $1,600 of your first $1,900 payment is interest — gone, exactly like rent
Property taxGone. Forever. Every year you own it
InsuranceGone
Mortgage insuranceGone — it protects the bank, not you
Repairs and maintenanceGone. The furnace, the roof, the water heater. A landlord pays these today; you will
Cost to buy and sellSeveral thousand to get in, and roughly 7% of the price to get out

So the honest sentence is: renting throws away 100% of the money. Buying throws away maybe 60–70% of it — at least in the early years — and keeps the rest. That's a real advantage. It's just not the total win people make it sound like.

Which means the real question is: how long?

Because it costs so much to buy and sell, you have to own long enough for the equity you build to out-run those costs. That's the break-even year. Sell before it, and you actually lose money — even if the house went up in value.

The Money tab calculates this for us specifically. Given our rent, our budget, and the price of the house, it tells you what year buying pulls ahead. If we might move before then, renting is genuinely the smarter financial move — not a failure.

And the reasons that aren't about money

You can paint the walls. Nobody can raise the rent or refuse to renew. You choose the school district. The payment mostly stops changing while rent keeps climbing. For a lot of people these matter more than the math — that's completely legitimate, as long as it's a choice you made on purpose rather than a thing you were talked into.

5. The Ohio thing nobody warns you aboutTwo of them, actually

Mixed school districts

In Franklin County, the city in your address is not always the school district you're in. There are Hilliard addresses that go to Dublin schools and Columbus addresses that go to Hilliard schools. Zillow will not tell you. This app checks the county's records on every home and stamps it ⚠️ MIXED DISTRICT when they don't match.

The tax bill goes up after you buy

Ohio charges property tax based on the county's valuation of the house, not what you paid. If you pay a lot more than the county's current number — which is normal — your tax bill climbs toward what you paid within a year or two. The app flags this on every home and tells you how much more to expect. This is the single most common nasty surprise for new owners.

7. Words people will use at youPlain English
EscrowThe account your lender uses to collect tax + insurance monthly and pay them for you. It's why your payment is bigger than the loan
PMI / MIPMortgage insurance. Protects the bank, not you. You pay it if you put under 20% down. It cancels on a conventional loan; on FHA it lasts the life of the loan
Earnest moneyA 1–2% deposit showing you're serious. Credited to you at closing. You get it back if you walk for a protected reason
ContingencyAn escape hatch in your offer. Do not waive these
Appraisal gapWhen the bank values the house below your offer. You cover the difference in cash, renegotiate, or walk
Closing costs2–4% of the price, on top of the down payment, to finalize the loan
EquityThe part of the house you actually own. This is the money that isn't "thrown away"
Pending / contingentSomeone's offer was accepted. Not worth chasing
